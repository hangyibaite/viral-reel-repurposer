# Long-form content rules (over 90 seconds)

For long-form content, argument flow dominates. Rhythm still matters, but the viewer is now committing to a thesis. If the argument breaks, they leave even if every line is funny.

## First, map the argument

Before swapping anything, write out the original's argument as a chain:

1. **The opening claim** — what does the speaker assert in the first 10-15 seconds?
2. **The setup** — what context establishes the claim as worth caring about?
3. **The evidence or examples** — what does the speaker show to make the claim land?
4. **The pivot** — where does the speaker turn from setup to thesis?
5. **The thesis / headline drop** — what is the speaker actually arguing?
6. **The implication** — what does the thesis mean for the viewer?
7. **The identity reframe** — who is the viewer now?
8. **The action** — what should the viewer do?

Write this chain out as 8 short lines before doing anything else. This is your map. Without it you cannot repurpose without breaking the argument.

## Then, mark the act breaks

Long-form content has natural act breaks (energy resets, topic transitions, audience re-entry points). Mark them by timestamp:

- Act 1: Setup → end of "why this matters" beat
- Act 2: Build / evidence → end of "here's what's actually happening" beat
- Act 3: Thesis + implication → end of headline drop
- Act 4: Action + close → CTA

Each act has its own runtime in the original. Match those runtimes (within ±10 seconds per act) in your new version. Do not let one act bloat and another shrink.

## Preserve argument structure even when content changes

When you swap the topic:

- The opening claim shifts to a NEW claim that fits the user's topic
- The setup shifts to context relevant to the new claim
- The evidence shifts to examples that prove the new claim
- The pivot stays in the same time slot
- The thesis is the new equivalent of the original's headline drop
- The implication tilts to the user's audience
- The identity reframe target swaps (e.g., "software engineer" → "marketer")
- The action becomes the user's CTA

But the FLOW is preserved. Claim → setup → evidence → pivot → thesis → implication → identity → action. Don't reorder. Don't skip a step.

## Logic gap rules

Every transition between sentences must make causal sense. In long-form, gaps are fatal because the viewer is tracking an argument.

When you swap content, re-read the transition between sentences and ask: does the new sentence follow from the previous one?

If not, fix the bridge. Use connectors:
- "and" — additive
- "because" — causal
- "so" — consequence
- "but" — pivot
- "which means" — implication
- "now" — temporal shift
- "the thing is" — meta-aside

Never leave a jagged jump. Even when content changes, the listener should never go "wait, why are we here?"

## The thesis line gets special treatment

In long-form, the thesis line (the "AI writes code, capisce?" beat) is the heart of the whole piece. Everything before sets it up. Everything after follows from it.

When repurposing:
- Find the thesis line in the original
- Identify its exact cadence (verb + noun + confirmation phrase)
- Build the new thesis to match that cadence
- Place it at the same timestamp ratio (e.g., if original thesis lands at 60% of runtime, new thesis lands at 60% of new runtime)
- Make it the hardest-hitting line in your new version, same as the original

## Audience recap moments

In long-form, the viewer's attention drifts. Good long-form scripts re-anchor periodically with phrases like:
- "Now, why do you need to care about this?"
- "Let me explain why this matters."
- "Stay with me, here's where it gets interesting."
- "If you're still with me..."

These are not filler. They are retention anchors. Preserve them at their timestamps. Don't strip them.

## Common long-form failures

- Collapsing the argument to "topic + tool list + CTA" → DON'T. The thesis still needs setup and implication.
- Moving the thesis earlier to "get to the point faster" → DON'T. The setup earns the thesis.
- Stripping recap moments to save time → DON'T. The viewer needs them.
- Letting one act bloat (usually the build) → DON'T. Compress proportionally.
- Breaking the causal chain when swapping content → DON'T. Re-read every transition.
- Cutting the implication beat ("now what does this mean for you") → DON'T. This is where the audience converts from listener to believer.

## Length math for long-form

Long-form runtimes scale by content density, not by topic. If the user wants more depth, allow more time. Don't compress just to be shorter.

But also: don't let it grow. A long-form viral piece is paced for its content. Adding content without removing content makes it drag.

When in doubt:
- Original was 1:50 → target 1:30 to 2:10
- Original was 3:00 → target 2:30 to 3:30
- Original was 5:00 → target 4:30 to 5:30

If the user explicitly asks for a specific runtime, hit it within ±5%.
